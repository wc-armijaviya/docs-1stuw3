Why the New Colorful Jubilee Rolex is Selling for Crazy Prices

Rolex has historically been conservative with color. Earth tones, blacks, whites, the occasional blue. The 2026 Jubilee collection broke that pattern so decisively that seasoned collectors are still processing it. Turquoise, coral, lavender — these are not limited editions. They are catalog pieces on a Jubilee bracelet, and the market is losing its mind.

The dials that started the frenzy

Six new lacquer dial colors debuted across the Datejust 36 and Datejust 41 in both Oystersteel and Rolesor configurations. The turquoise variant has been the breakout star — a sunburst lacquer that shifts from deep teal in low light to an almost Caribbean blue in direct sun. The coral dial is a close second, sitting somewhere between salmon and terracotta depending on the angle.

Why the Jubilee bracelet matters

Rolex's five-link Jubilee bracelet was introduced in 1945 for the original Datejust. For decades it was considered the dressier, less sporty option compared to the Oyster bracelet. That perception reversed completely over the past five years. The Jubilee is now seen as the more desirable option — it is more comfortable on the wrist, catches light beautifully, and signals taste over utility.

Production and availability

Rolex has not confirmed production numbers, but AD allocation patterns suggest the colorful dials are being produced at roughly the same volume as standard Datejust configurations. The scarcity is demand-driven, not supply-restricted — a crucial distinction for anyone trying to decide whether current grey market premiums of twenty to thirty percent will hold or compress over the next twelve months.
